# Writing Rules

## Scope

- For the next 1-2 months, the top-level goal is to raise readers' understanding of AI and help them see how AI can truly enter an individual workflow.
- Prefer topics that clarify how people should actually work with AI, not just what AI can do in theory.
- Use these topic pillars as the default content boundary:
  - how AI truly enters a personal workflow
  - common wrong moves in AI workflows
  - what should stay with the human vs. be handed to AI
  - the real infrastructure or conditions required for AI to keep working
- Treat interaction lift as the main optimization target, but do not chase interaction with off-theme topics that drift away from the workflow mission.
- When choosing between several acceptable topics, prefer the one that is closest to a real work decision, a real workflow mistake, or a real friction point the reader can immediately map to daily work.
- Current weekly mother question: `当 AI 开始进入工作流后，人最容易在哪些地方判断失误。`
- For the current week, daily topics should be different cuts of that mother question rather than unrelated standalone ideas.
- Avoid repeating the recently crowded angles in the current week:
  - AI 只是聊天框
  - AI 进不了工作流
  - 没有记忆
  - 过早接管
  - bash / sandbox / 边界
  - 治理成本

## Body

- Before changing any local sentence, title, or image, first check whether the draft still matches the account's current stable style and recent article family.
- Protect global consistency before optimizing local wording.
- Passing the body means more than being logically correct. It still has to read like natural human Chinese, like a finished piece, and like something actually worth publishing.
- Rewrite any line that sounds like an internal compressed phrase the writer understands but a normal reader would never say out loud.
- If a sentence is accurate but still reads like machine-facing or author-facing language, it is not done.
- The article should state the final judgment directly, not replay the conversation.
- Before drafting, identify the real center of gravity and keep title, body, headings, and images aligned to it.
- The account should strengthen a recognizable position around AI-era individual upgrading and the path toward a one-person company.
- Short judgment post: default to about 500 Chinese characters, 3-4 compact sections, one sharp judgment, one strong example or analogy, one strong close.
- Deep explanatory post: prioritize clarity, structure, and full explanation when the topic is a framework, layered model, or technical evolution.
- If a short judgment post still needs a real example, one conceptual distinction, and one consequence layer to make sense, 500-700 Chinese characters is usually too short; expand until the judgment is earned.
- If a topic depends on explaining why one state is more dangerous than another and how the problem propagates through later steps, 500 Chinese characters is usually not enough; prefer roughly 800-1000 Chinese characters.
- Do not let the opening hook steal the article's center of gravity.
- Headings must carry information, not writing-process commentary.
- When writing a practical how-to article, cut to the setup or process quickly; do not spend the opening paragraphs explaining that you are about to explain it.
- When the problem is structure, fix thesis, paragraph roles, heading quality, and image purpose before adding more material.
- Do not hand the user a structural half-draft.
- Do not compress a draft into its final short form until it has: one concrete example, one explicit explanation layer, and one clean closing judgment.
- Do not treat a strong sentence as a finished article.
- A strong judgment line is only the opening claim. It does not count as a finished draft until the article has earned it with enough concrete explanation.
- When the user asks to rewrite, rebuild the whole article around the final judgment instead of patching paragraph by paragraph.
- A rewrite means whole-article reconstruction. Do not try to rescue a weak draft through sequential local patches.
- If one concrete case is used as the opening hook, do not let it swallow the article; move from the case to the wider reader-facing pattern early.
- When broadening from a concrete case to a larger point, avoid stiff authorial transition lines; the turn should feel like the argument unfolding, not the writer stepping out to explain the structure.
- When a new article is a tight follow-up to the previous day's piece, it may briefly reference or link back to that earlier article to preserve continuity. Do not re-explain yesterday's core argument at length.
- In a follow-up article, cut to today's new move quickly. The reader should feel the piece is advancing the discussion, not replaying the last post.
- For follow-up articles, lock today's center of gravity first. Do not let yesterday's framing, checklist, or categories drag the new article off its own core point.
- If the new article has its own primary value, do not force an obvious 1:1 mapping to yesterday's structure just to create continuity.
- In a step-by-step article, each numbered step must add new information. If two steps are doing the same work, merge or rewrite them.
- In a practical article, show the real artifact when it helps more than paraphrasing it. Prefer a screenshot or concrete file snippet over repeating the same process in prose.
- Background from the user is for understanding; only keep what strengthens the reader-facing argument.
- When a general公众号 reader may not know an acronym, write the full term first.
- Treat evidence-standard, compliance, and tone instructions as writing constraints, not article sentences.
- Avoid habitual spoken filler and backstage framing.
- Do not let repeated sentence templates become a crutch. If a 700-character article already contains multiple `不是……而是……` turns, rewrite them; one pattern should not dominate the piece.
- Do not let a fixed rhetorical template substitute for real writing. If the same contrast sentence pattern starts carrying the article, rewrite the affected paragraphs instead of polishing individual lines.
- Keep backstage writing-analysis language out of the body. Phrases like "core judgment", "hook", or "example and conclusion" belong in review, not in reader-facing prose.
- AI flavor usually comes from abstract summary language, not from the presence of important concepts. Keep the key concept if it is the article's real value, but write the surrounding sentences in plain human language.
- Start from a real thing worth saying, then organize it. Do not start from a structurally correct explanation and fill it with meaning afterward.
- Prefer direct actions, felt friction, and concrete consequences over explanatory summary phrases.
- Be suspicious of sentences that sound like a writer presenting a method, framework, or article structure rather than a person talking about what actually happens.
- If a sentence is accurate but sounds like something only the writer would say, rewrite it into language a reader might naturally use.
- Watch for abstract filler nouns such as `结果`, `问题`, `机制`, `价值`, `完成线` when a more concrete description would read more naturally.
- Avoid writerly posture like `今天这篇再往前走一步`, `这套架构最先解决的是`, or similar phrasing that explains the article instead of the thing itself.
- Optimize for `this sounds like someone who really does it` rather than `this sounds like a smart explanation of why it works`.
- Do not confuse core concepts with AI flavor. If `Agent`, `Generator`, `Evaluator`, or other key concepts are the article's real value, keep them; remove the AI flavor from the surrounding sentences instead.
- When the article is about a practical setup or workflow, write it as `why I had to do this` or `what kept going wrong without it`, not as a product or method explainer.
- If the sentence feels compressed, clever, or abstract in the writer's head, expand or rewrite it into plain spoken language before keeping it.
- In practical articles, avoid meta-writing phrases such as `今天就谈谈`, `这篇讲的是`, `最先解决的是`, or similar author commentary when a direct statement would do.
- Prefer concrete project language over vague containers. If the user is talking about an `agent 项目`, do not drift back to `这个文件夹` unless the file-system container itself is the point.
- In practical articles, do not treat structure and images as later polish. Headings, emphasis, and images are part of the first-class draft.

## TL;DR

- TL;DR should summarize today's article only.
- Do not use TL;DR to replay or explain yesterday's article.
- Do not use author-facing framing like `这篇讲的是`, `昨天提到`, or similar commentary in TL;DR.
- TL;DR should state the key judgment or key move directly in plain Chinese.
- TL;DR should not read like config notes, review language, or a methods memo.
- TL;DR should stay aligned with the body and should not introduce points the article does not actually develop.
- TL;DR should be compressed, but it still needs to say what problem this piece is solving and what the main answer is.
- For practical articles, TL;DR should usually start from the action or setup itself, not from `我怎么...` or other self-referential framing.
- TL;DR is a deliverable, not a side note. It should stand on its own as a readable summary sentence or two.

## Title

- Titles should say the human meaning first; keep jargon restrained.
- Titles must carry a real judgment, tension, or consequence. Do not use a title that merely restates the topic label.
- Reject titles that are flat, over-explaining, or read like the article's conclusion. Do not let a merely accurate title survive review.
- Review titles for click-worthiness, not just correctness.

## Cover Text

- Cover text belongs to packaging, but it still has to stay aligned with the article's center of gravity.
- Keep cover text short, usually within about 4 Chinese characters or an equivalent-length English phrase, and judgment-like instead of a bare noun chunk or sentence fragment.
- Treat cover text and inline image copy as deliverables, not optional extras.
- Image text should still make sense when separated from the article body.
- An inline image is not finished just because it matches the article; it should also be worth sharing on its own.
- Before rewriting image copy, first verify that the image style still matches the project's current visual language and the recent batch of article images.

## Review Standard

- Every draft should have at least one sentence strong enough to save or forward.
- For deep posts, explicitly check: hook stealing focus, repeated hook restatement, empty headings, decorative images.
- Unscheduled drafts must be publishable on their own and should not rely on relative timing like `昨天/前天/上一期`.
- Before calling a short judgment post finished, verify that the conclusion is not merely stated but actually explained and earned.
- Do not force a piece into a 500-character shape if the topic still requires one more explanation layer to be truly understandable.
- If a follow-up article proposes a reason for the previous article's problem, verify that the reason actually fits the previous article's judgment. Do not invent a simpler causal story that misreads the prior piece.
- If the article's core idea is practical but the draft reads like a framework description, rewrite it. Practical value should feel lived, not presented.
- In a practical article, review the TL;DR, headings, numbered steps, title, and cover text as independent deliverables. Do not only review the body paragraphs.
- Review in this order: style consistency first, then structure completeness, then natural language, then local optimization.
